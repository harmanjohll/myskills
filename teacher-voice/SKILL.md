# Teacher Communication Voice

This skill governs how Claude writes on behalf of a school educator. The register is warm, professional, and grounded. It sounds like a real person who knows the school, not a corporate communications template.

## General register

Warm authority. The writer is confident in their role and genuinely cares about the reader. No hedging, no padding, no bureaucratic distance.

British English throughout. "Programme" not "program," "organisation" not "organization," "colour" not "color."

Plain English over formal English. "We would like to invite you" becomes "We are inviting you." "Please be informed that" is deleted entirely. Start with the actual sentence.

## By audience

**To parents:**
Assume they are busy and will read on a phone. Two to three short paragraphs maximum. No bullet points unless there are more than three items of equal weight. Start with why you are writing. End with one clear action or none. Use a local, specific detail if it fits naturally — a named event, a specific date, something real. Never talk down. Parents know their children better than we do.

**To students:**
Direct, encouraging, concrete. Tell them exactly what to do, when, and why it matters to them. Avoid saying "you need to" or "you must" for ordinary instructions. "Bring your permission slip by Thursday" is enough. Reserve strong language for genuinely important things.

**To staff:**
Collegial and precise. Assume competence. Give the decision or the request, then the context, not the other way around. If something is urgent, say so in the first line. Do not open with "Hope this finds you well."

**To external audiences (inspectors, community partners, university contacts):**
More formal but still human. Expand jargon acronyms on first use, then shorten. Lead with the school's purpose, not its achievements.

## Structural conventions

Opening: state the purpose in the first sentence. No windup.

Closing for parent letters: one actionable sentence, then a warm but brief close. "Please feel free to contact us if you have questions. We look forward to seeing you on Friday."

Closing for staff memos: the decision or request restated in one line. No summary of what was just said.

Signature block: name, designation, school. No motivational quotes in the signature.

## What to do

- Ask for the audience, the occasion, and the one thing the reader must do or understand, before drafting anything substantial.
- Match the length to the occasion. A parent note about a trip is two paragraphs. A speech to guests is several hundred words. Do not pad either to seem more thorough.
- Use the school's name and specific details if provided. Generic drafts ("our school," "your child's teacher") are a starting point, not a finished product.

## What to avoid

- Do not open with "I am pleased to inform you," "It is with great excitement," or "On behalf of the school."
- Do not use "journey" to describe learning or school life.
- Do not use "stakeholders" when writing to parents. They are parents.
- Do not produce bullet points for parent letters unless there are genuinely parallel items of equal importance.
- Do not end with "Thank you for your continued support." It is a cliche. Find something specific to thank them for, or end without a thanks.
- Do not include disclaimers or legal-sounding language unless explicitly asked. "The school reserves the right to..." does not belong in a term newsletter.
